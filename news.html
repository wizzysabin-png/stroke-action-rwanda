World Stroke Day 2025: A Huge Success in Kigali

February 01, 2021

StrokeAction RNCDA Workshop_2021

On November 13, 2021, Stroke Action Rwanda delegated two of its members to participate in a workshop at Galaxy Hotel, organized under the ‘Our Views, Our Voices’ initiative. This global program fosters the meaningful engagement of people living with noncommunicable diseases (NCDs) in shaping health policies and responses. It empowers participants to share lived experiences, advocate for improved care, influence decision-making, and contribute to the reduction of stigma and discrimination. The workshop was convened by the Rwanda NCD Alliance to enhance knowledge and capacity in NCDadvocacy andpolicy engagement.

Survivor Profile: Hope After Recovery

My name is Alexia Uwajeneza, and I am 46 years old. I have been living with the effects of a stroke for more than 12.5 years. Before my stroke, I lived a full and stable life. I had a happy family and worked as a midwife. On Sunday, December 19, 2010, at approximately 8:00 a.m., while rushing to attend the first Mass, I suddenly felt extreme weakness and severe dizziness. I lost consciousness shortly afterward. My husband immediately took me to the nearest private clinic in Muhanga, where I received first aid and briefly regained consciousness. Due to the severity of my condition, I was referred to Kabgayi Hospital. When doctors there were unable to determine the cause of my symptoms, I was referred again to CHUK. Unfortunately, due to overcrowding, I had to wait for an available room. During this time, I stayed with a friend who lived near the hospital.

How Your Donations Provided 50 Wheelchairs

Thanks to your generous donations, 50 individuals living with mobility challenges—many of them stroke survivors—have received wheelchairs that are transforming their daily lives. Your support made it possible to identify those most in need, purchase quality wheelchairs, and deliver them directly to patients and families who previously struggled with limited or no mobility support. For many recipients, these wheelchairs mean more than movement—they represent dignity, independence, and renewed hope. Beneficiaries can now move safely within their homes, attend medical appointments, participate in community life, and rely less on others for basic daily activities. Your kindness didn’t just provide wheelchairs—it restored independence and hope. 💙
